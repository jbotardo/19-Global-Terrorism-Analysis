Data Analysis

Where is the data for 1993?

The original PGIS data, whereupon the 1970-1997 GTD information are based, consisted of hard-copy index cards originally. When being electronically transferred by researchers, significant data for 1993 went missing and is omitted due to this.

Database Debacles

Data type for loading in MySQL, INT and DOUBLE cannot take negative. "Solution: INT Signed, DOUBLE signed" But later we did not end up using this as MySQL Data Import would give a lot of issues, and we changed the approach to using Pandas to load the data.

Cleaning up the Data

We decided to only take data from the current century (2000-2016) to help performance. Entries that were missing important tools such as lattitude and longitude were removed for mapping purposes. After cleaning with PANDAS, the data was loaded back into mySQL with 99,801 rows.

Checked for Accuracy

For the integrity of our information, we cross-referenced our datasets by querying major terrorism events (September 11th, Pulse Nightclub, Etc.) from our database, and comparing our outcomes to official news reports to match correct values.

Data Summary

Based on our analysis of worldwide terrorism, we discovered a possible trend in casualties using machine learning and linear regression. From 2000 to 2011, acts of terrorism increased slightly over the years 2000 to 2011, but in 2012 there was a massive increase in terrorist activity. The increase in 2012 is due to factors such as religion, economics, and political disputes. Since 2012, global terrorism has been on a rise, and the casualty count is continuing to rise year after year. Casualty is defined as a person killed or injured by a war or an accident, so our analysis includes those both wounded and killed by terrorist attacks. While the above graphs fluctuate up and down with the number of casualties, we applied the linear regression machine learning model to train a Python program to identify a linear trend. Our model was able to find a trend line for the number wounded with ~71% accuracy, and a trend line for the number killed with ~75% accuracy. Both trend lines appear as the dotted lines in the graph above. The important thing to note is while 2016 is lower than 2015 in terms of number of casualties, the trend line is predicting these counts to rise, based on years of past data.

What started the spike in terrorism from 2012-2016?

Starting in 2012, there is a massive spike in the number of recored terrorist events worldwide. From 4941 attacks in 2011, to almost double in that amount (8077) in 2012. According to the U.S. Department of State, in 2012 the al-Qa'ida core in Afghanistan and Pakistan was severly weakened. However, operating out of safe havens, they were able to form close ties with other major terrorist sects such as the Taliban, and began to aggressively spread operations across southern and central Asia.

In other regions, a large contribution to this massive increase starting in 2012 comes from Greece of all places, where anarchists began to carry out terrorist attacks on political targets and sites as the economy of Greece continued to spiral out of control. The U.S. Department of State claims 226 attacks in Greece in November alone, while Reuters reports 527 arson and bomb attacks in 2012 for Greece alone. From 1974 to 1986, Greece had a reported 542 arson and bomb attacks for that 12 year span. For the massive spike in 2012-2016, a large amount of that can be pointed at Greece, as its anarchist revolution over the failing economy really took off in 2012.

In Colombia in 2012, clashing between the government and Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colmbia (FARC) greatly intensified. Even a temporary ceasefire from November of 2012 to January of 2013 did not slow down the rapid growth of attacks, with the U.S. Department of State reporting a 52% increase in attacks over 2011. Most of the attacks were carried out on political sites, police stations, or the military; however, a significant amount amount of casualties were civilian. This 52% increase is another factor in our spike from 2012-2016, and continuing political disputes between FARC and the Colombian government have only lead to increasing acts of terrorism in South America.
